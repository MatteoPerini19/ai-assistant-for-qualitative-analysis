DEFINITION OF PSYCHOLOGICAL DEPTH
Psychological depth is the extent to which a thinker:
- surfaces and interrogates foundational, often tacit, beliefs or values organizing wide swathes of cognition
- coordinates multiple, distinct facets of an issue into an internally coherent whole by both differentiating them and integrating them together

CONCEPTUAL STRUCTURE
- Overall depth/profundity is the broad construct to be rated.
- Engagement with fundamental principles is one component of psychological depth.
- Complexity, or integrative complexity, is another component of psychological depth.
- Complexity has two distinguishable components: differentiation and integration.
- A reflection can be high on differentiation but lower on integration, or vice versa.
- High overall depth typically requires at least some strength in both foundational engagement and complexity.

GENERAL RATING RULES
- Score the reflection, not the writer.

- Do not inflate scores because the topic sounds serious.

- Differentiate differentiation from integration: listing multiple aspects without connecting them is not the same as integrating them.
- Integration requires actual linkage or synthesis.
- Use the strongest textual evidence, but base the score on the reflection as a whole.
- Overall depth/profundity should not be reduced to one single feature such as emotional intensity, confession, or abstraction.

High scores require more than emotional intensity or abstract wording. They typically involve both:

4. INTEGRATION OF IDEAS
Integration is the second component of complexity, or integrative complexity. It concerns whether distinct ideas are connected into a coherent whole, underlying structure, or larger pattern.

Use the following item statements as semantic anchors:
- "I integrated various elements or perspectives into a coherent overall picture of the issue."
- "I linked different ideas together to uncover an underlying structure or larger pattern."
- "My reflection showed no attempt to connect various ideas into a coherent whole." (R)

Interpretation:
This dimension concerns whether the reflection actually links its distinct elements together through synthesis, pattern detection, overarching structure, causal organization, or coherent framing.

Anchor points:
- 1: The reflection presents disconnected points, or no real attempt at synthesis.
- 4: Some links or partial coherence are present, but integration is limited, local, or incomplete.
- 7: The reflection clearly connects distinct ideas into a coherent, organized, and illuminating whole.

IMPORTANT DISTINCTIONS
- A reflection may be high in differentiation but lower in integration if it raises many distinct points without connecting them.
- A reflection may show some coherence yet still be low in differentiation if it develops only one narrow line of thought.
- Emotional intensity, confessional openness, abstraction, eloquence, or metaphor use do not by themselves demonstrate differentiation or integration.
- A reflection can discuss very serious material yet still be shallow if it remains descriptive, routine, slogan-like, or conceptually one-dimensional.
